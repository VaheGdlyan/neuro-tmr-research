# RQ-015

## Question

How are sleep stages identified and measured?

---

## Status

**Answered**

---

## Why this question matters

A future Targeted Memory Reactivation (TMR) system must determine the user's current sleep stage before delivering any sensory cue. Since different stages of sleep support different biological processes, accurate sleep-stage identification is essential for ensuring that external stimulation occurs at the appropriate physiological moment.

Understanding how sleep stages are objectively measured provides the scientific foundation for future sleep-stage detection algorithms and consumer wearable technologies.

---

## Write the answer in your own words with Scientific evidence

The **gold standard** for identifying and measuring sleep stages is **polysomnography (PSG)**, a comprehensive sleep-monitoring technique performed in sleep laboratories. Rather than relying on a single physiological signal, polysomnography combines multiple synchronized measurements to determine the brain's current physiological state throughout the night.

The three primary signals used for sleep-stage classification are:

### Electroencephalography (EEG)

Electroencephalography (EEG) records the electrical activity generated by large populations of synchronized cortical neurons through electrodes placed on the scalp. EEG provides the primary information used for identifying sleep stages because each stage is characterized by distinctive patterns of brain activity.

Examples include:

- **Wakefulness:** predominantly alpha and beta activity.
- **N1:** transition from alpha to theta activity.
- **N2:** appearance of sleep spindles and K-complexes.
- **N3 (Slow-Wave Sleep):** high-amplitude, low-frequency delta waves.
- **REM sleep:** low-voltage, desynchronized activity resembling wakefulness.

These characteristic EEG patterns allow clinicians to distinguish the different stages of sleep.

---

### Electrooculography (EOG)

Electrooculography (EOG) records eye movements throughout sleep.

Eye movement patterns differ substantially between sleep stages. Slow rolling eye movements are commonly observed during the transition into sleep (N1), while **rapid eye movements** are the defining physiological characteristic of REM sleep. Recording eye movements therefore provides important complementary information for distinguishing REM from Non-REM sleep.

---

### Electromyography (EMG)

Electromyography (EMG) records skeletal muscle activity, usually from the chin muscles.

Muscle tone gradually decreases as sleep deepens and reaches its lowest level during REM sleep, where generalized muscle atonia prevents the body from physically acting out dreams. EMG therefore plays a critical role in distinguishing REM sleep from other sleep stages.

---

### Sleep Stage Classification

Sleep stages are not identified using a single physiological measurement. Instead, trained sleep specialists analyze the combined patterns observed in EEG, EOG, and EMG according to standardized clinical scoring criteria, such as those established by the **American Academy of Sleep Medicine (AASM)**.

Using these combined physiological signals, each period of sleep is classified as:

- Wake
- N1
- N2
- N3 (Slow-Wave Sleep)
- REM

This integrated approach provides the highest accuracy for determining the current physiological state of the sleeping brain.

---

### Beyond the Sleep Laboratory

In addition to EEG, EOG, and EMG, clinical polysomnography may also include measurements such as electrocardiography (ECG), respiration, blood oxygen saturation (SpO₂), airflow, body position, and limb movements. These additional physiological signals help evaluate overall sleep quality and diagnose sleep disorders but are not the primary indicators used for basic sleep-stage classification.

Outside the laboratory, most consumer wearable devices cannot directly measure brain activity. Instead, they estimate sleep stages using indirect physiological signals such as heart rate, heart-rate variability (HRV), body movement (actigraphy), respiration, skin temperature, and blood oxygen levels. Although these methods are generally less accurate than polysomnography, they provide practical solutions for continuous sleep monitoring in everyday environments.

---

## Scientific Conclusion

Sleep stages are identified primarily through **polysomnography (PSG)**, the clinical gold standard for sleep monitoring. PSG combines EEG, EOG, and EMG to evaluate synchronized brain activity, eye movements, and muscle tone, allowing accurate classification of wakefulness, N1, N2, N3, and REM sleep. Consumer wearable devices estimate these stages indirectly using physiological signals rather than directly measuring brain activity.

## Why this matters for our product

This question establishes one of the most important engineering foundations of the project.

The future TMR system will not directly observe memory consolidation. Instead, it must first determine the user's current sleep stage by interpreting measurable physiological signals.

In laboratory environments, polysomnography provides highly accurate sleep-stage classification through direct measurement of brain activity. However, a practical consumer TMR system cannot rely on laboratory-grade EEG equipment. Instead, it will likely estimate sleep stages using wearable physiological signals such as heart rate, HRV, respiration, movement, skin temperature, and other non-invasive measurements.

One of the major engineering challenges of this project will therefore be determining whether consumer-accessible physiological signals can approximate laboratory-grade sleep-stage detection with sufficient accuracy to enable reliable and safe Targeted Memory Reactivation.
