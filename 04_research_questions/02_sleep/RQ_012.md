# RQ-012

## Question

What are the stages of sleep, and what happens during each stage?

---

## Status

**Answered**

---

## Why this question matters

Sleep is not a single, uniform physiological state. Instead, it consists of multiple stages that differ in their patterns of brain activity, physiological characteristics, and biological functions. Since each sleep stage supports different processes within the brain, understanding this architecture is essential before investigating memory consolidation and, later, Targeted Memory Reactivation (TMR).

By understanding what occurs during each stage, we can identify the biological conditions under which different cognitive and physiological processes take place.

---

## Write the answer in your own words with Scientific evidence

Human sleep is organized into a repeating sequence of **Non-Rapid Eye Movement (NREM)** and **Rapid Eye Movement (REM)** sleep. These stages alternate throughout the night in cycles that typically last between **90 and 120 minutes**, with each cycle contributing differently to brain function and physiological regulation.

### Stage N1 — Transition from Wakefulness to Sleep

N1 is the lightest stage of sleep and represents the transition from wakefulness into stable sleep. During this stage, the brain gradually shifts from the alpha-wave activity characteristic of relaxed wakefulness to slower theta-wave activity. Muscle tone begins to decrease, slow eye movements appear, and responsiveness to external stimuli is reduced.

Although consciousness begins to fade, individuals in N1 can still be awakened easily. This stage primarily serves as the brain's transition into the sleeping state and prepares the nervous system for deeper stages of sleep.

---

### Stage N2 — Stable Non-REM Sleep

N2 represents the largest proportion of total sleep in healthy adults. It is characterized by two distinctive EEG features: **sleep spindles** and **K-complexes**.

During this stage, the brain becomes less responsive to external sensory input while maintaining stable sleep. Sleep spindles and K-complexes are believed to contribute to maintaining sleep continuity, filtering unnecessary sensory information, and supporting early neural processes involved in learning and memory.

Compared with N1, awakening from N2 requires a greater level of stimulation, reflecting the brain's progression toward deeper sleep.

---

### Stage N3 — Slow-Wave Sleep (SWS)

N3, also known as **Slow-Wave Sleep (SWS)** or deep sleep, is the deepest stage of Non-REM sleep. It is characterized by high-amplitude, low-frequency **delta waves**, indicating highly synchronized neuronal activity across the cerebral cortex.

This stage has the highest arousal threshold, making individuals difficult to awaken. During Slow-Wave Sleep, the brain and body enter their deepest restorative state, supporting numerous physiological processes associated with recovery, metabolic regulation, hormonal activity, and the strengthening of newly acquired memories.

Because of these unique biological characteristics, Slow-Wave Sleep plays a central role in maintaining normal cognitive and physiological function.

---

### REM Sleep — Rapid Eye Movement Sleep

REM sleep differs fundamentally from all stages of NREM sleep. During REM, brain activity becomes highly active and desynchronized, producing EEG patterns that closely resemble those observed during wakefulness. At the same time, skeletal muscles become almost completely paralyzed (muscle atonia), while rapid eye movements and vivid dreaming commonly occur.

Although the brain remains highly active during REM sleep, its functional role differs from that of NREM sleep. REM is strongly associated with emotional processing, procedural learning, and the integration of newly acquired experiences into existing neural networks.

The combination of high brain activity and muscle atonia has led REM sleep to be described as an "active brain in a resting body."

---

Together, these four stages form the normal architecture of human sleep. Rather than representing separate physiological events, they function as complementary components of a repeating biological cycle in which the brain continuously changes its patterns of activity to support different cognitive, neurological, and physiological functions throughout the night.

---

## Scientific Conclusion

Human sleep is composed of four physiological stages: **N1, N2, N3 (Slow-Wave Sleep), and REM sleep**. Each stage possesses distinct patterns of brain activity, characteristic physiological features, and specialized biological functions. N1 serves as the transition into sleep, N2 stabilizes sleep and reduces responsiveness to external stimuli, N3 provides the deepest restorative state of sleep, and REM supports highly active brain function associated with dreaming and complex cognitive processing. Together, these stages form a repeating sleep architecture that enables normal brain function and overall physiological health.
