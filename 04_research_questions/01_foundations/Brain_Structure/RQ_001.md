## Write the answer in your own words with Scientific evidence

The human brain has a hierarchical architecture composed of several major regions that work together as one interconnected biological system. Although each region has its own primary responsibilities, none of them works in isolation. They constantly communicate through networks of neurons.

The largest part of the brain is the **cerebrum**, which consists of the **left and right cerebral hemispheres**. These hemispheres are connected by a large bundle of neuronal fibers called the **corpus callosum**, allowing both sides of the brain to exchange information. The outer visible layer of the cerebrum is the **cerebral cortex**, which performs many of the higher cognitive functions of the human brain.

The cerebral cortex is divided into four major lobes, each specializing in different tasks:

- **Frontal lobe** – responsible for planning, reasoning, decision making, voluntary movement, and other executive functions.
- **Parietal lobe** – integrates sensory information from the body and helps us perceive touch, space, and body position.
- **Occipital lobe** – the primary center for visual processing.
- **Temporal lobe** – responsible for auditory processing, language comprehension, object and facial recognition, and plays a major role in learning and memory.

Looking deeper inside the brain, several important structures are located beneath the cerebral cortex.

The **hippocampus** is one of the key structures responsible for forming and encoding new memories. Next to it lies the **amygdala**, which assigns emotional significance to experiences and memories. Another important structure is the **thalamus**, which acts as the brain's major sensory relay station by receiving information from the senses and transmitting it to different cortical regions. Beneath the thalamus is the **hypothalamus**, which regulates many essential physiological processes and communicates with the body through the **pituitary gland**, controlling hormonal functions and maintaining homeostasis.

Many of these deep structures participate in the **limbic system**, a network involved in emotion, motivation, learning, and memory. Together with the cerebral cortex, they form the majority of the **forebrain**.

Below the forebrain lies the **midbrain**, which participates in eye movements, visual and auditory reflexes, motor coordination, and contains important groups of neurons involved in movement and other functions. It closely interacts with structures such as the basal ganglia that help regulate voluntary movement.

The lowest major division is the **hindbrain**, which includes the **cerebellum**, **pons**, and **medulla**.

The **cerebellum**, located beneath the occipital lobe, is the second largest part of the brain and plays an important role in motor coordination, balance, precision of movement, and motor learning.

The **pons** contributes to breathing, posture, sleep regulation, and communication between different parts of the brain.

The **medulla oblongata** connects the brain to the spinal cord and controls many vital automatic functions, including breathing, heart rate, and blood pressure.

Together, the **midbrain**, **pons**, and **medulla** form the **brainstem**, which is responsible for many of the body's essential life-supporting functions.

Communication throughout the brain occurs through **neurons**, which transmit electrical and chemical signals to one another. Networks of interconnected neurons allow different brain regions to exchange information and coordinate complex behaviors.

One example of this communication is the interaction between the **thalamus** and the **cerebral cortex**. Their continuous exchange of signals generates rhythmic electrical activity that can be measured using **electroencephalography (EEG)**. These rhythmic patterns are known as **brain waves**.

Different brain wave frequencies are associated with different brain states:

- **Alpha waves (8–13 Hz)** – typically observed during relaxed wakefulness, especially when the eyes are closed.
- **Beta waves (14–30 Hz)** – associated with active thinking, concentration, and focused mental activity.
- **Theta waves (4–7 Hz)** – commonly observed during light sleep and some memory-related processes.
- **Delta waves (below approximately 4 Hz)** – characteristic of deep Slow-Wave Sleep (SWS).

Neural communication is maintained by two major classes of neurons. Approximately **80%** are **excitatory neurons**, which increase the activity of other neurons and propagate signals through neural circuits. Approximately **20%** are **inhibitory neurons**, which suppress neural activity and maintain stability, preventing excessive or uncontrolled excitation within brain networks.

Overall, the human brain can be understood as a highly organized, distributed biological system composed of specialized regions that communicate continuously through complex neural networks. This organization allows different brain structures to perform specialized functions while working together to produce perception, learning, memory, movement, emotion, and consciousness.
