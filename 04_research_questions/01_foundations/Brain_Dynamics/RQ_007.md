# RQ-007

## Question

What are brain oscillations, and why do they occur?

---

## Status

**Answered**

## Why this question matters

Targeted Memory Reactivation depends on delivering sensory cues during very specific stages of sleep. These sleep stages are identified primarily through characteristic patterns of brain oscillations measured with electroencephalography (EEG). Understanding brain oscillations is therefore essential for recognizing when the brain is in the optimal state for memory consolidation.

---

## Write the answer in your own words with Scientific evidence

Brain oscillations are rhythmic patterns of synchronized electrical activity generated by large populations of neurons communicating with one another. These oscillations arise because millions of excitatory and inhibitory neurons continuously exchange electrical and chemical signals, producing rhythmic activity that can be detected outside the skull using electroencephalography (EEG).

Different patterns of oscillatory activity correspond to different functional states of the brain. Scientists classify these oscillations into frequency bands known as **brain waves**, each associated with characteristic cognitive or physiological conditions.

The major brain wave types are:

- **Delta waves (0.5–4 Hz)** – dominant during deep Slow-Wave Sleep (SWS) and associated with memory consolidation and physical recovery.
- **Theta waves (4–8 Hz)** – commonly observed during light sleep, memory processing, and certain learning-related states.
- **Alpha waves (8–13 Hz)** – present during relaxed wakefulness, especially when the eyes are closed.
- **Beta waves (13–30 Hz)** – associated with active thinking, attention, and focused mental activity.
- **Gamma waves (above 30 Hz)** – linked to higher cognitive processing, perception, and information integration.

Brain oscillations occur because neurons rarely function independently. Instead, they operate as coordinated neural networks whose synchronized activity produces rhythmic electrical patterns. The frequency of these oscillations reflects the current functional state of the brain, allowing researchers to distinguish wakefulness, different sleep stages, attention, and other cognitive processes.

---

## Scientific Conclusion

Brain oscillations are synchronized electrical rhythms generated by communicating neural networks. Their frequency changes according to the brain's functional state and can be measured non-invasively using EEG. Different oscillatory patterns serve as reliable biological markers for identifying wakefulness and the various stages of sleep.

## Why this matters for our product

Brain oscillations provide one of the primary biological signals that allow a TMR system to determine **when** a memory cue should be delivered.

Our future system will not attempt to identify sleep stages by guesswork. Instead, it will rely on physiological signals that reflect underlying brain oscillations. In particular, the appearance of dominant **delta-wave activity** is one of the strongest indicators that the user has entered Slow-Wave Sleep, the stage during which memory consolidation and successful Targeted Memory Reactivation are believed to occur.

Understanding brain oscillations therefore provides the scientific basis for detecting the optimal timing of cue presentation in a future consumer TMR system.
