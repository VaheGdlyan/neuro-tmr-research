# RQ-009

## Question

How are brain oscillations measured, and what physiological information do they provide?

---

## Status

**Answered**

---

## Why this question matters

A future Targeted Memory Reactivation (TMR) system must determine the user's current brain state before delivering sensory cues. To accomplish this, the system requires reliable physiological measurements that indicate whether the brain is awake, in light sleep, or in Slow-Wave Sleep (SWS). Understanding how brain oscillations are measured provides the foundation for future sleep-stage detection algorithms.

---

## Write the answer in your own words with Scientific evidence

Brain oscillations are measured primarily using **Electroencephalography (EEG)**, a non-invasive technique that records the electrical activity generated by large populations of neurons. Small electrodes placed on the scalp detect tiny voltage fluctuations produced by synchronized neuronal activity within the cerebral cortex.

Although EEG does not measure the activity of individual neurons, it captures the collective electrical behavior of millions of neurons firing together. These synchronized electrical patterns appear as brain waves with characteristic frequencies and amplitudes.

The measured oscillations provide important physiological information about the functional state of the brain. Different patterns indicate whether a person is awake, concentrating, relaxing, dreaming, or sleeping deeply.

For example:

- **Beta waves** are commonly associated with active thinking and focused attention.
- **Alpha waves** appear during relaxed wakefulness.
- **Theta waves** become more prominent during light sleep.
- **Delta waves** dominate during Slow-Wave Sleep (SWS), indicating deep sleep and active memory consolidation.
- **Gamma waves** are associated with higher cognitive processing and information integration.

By analyzing these oscillatory patterns, researchers can classify sleep stages, monitor cognitive states, detect neurological disorders, and investigate how brain activity changes during learning and memory formation.

In addition to EEG, sleep laboratories often combine several physiological measurements during **polysomnography (PSG)**. These include eye movements (EOG), muscle activity (EMG), heart rate (ECG), respiration, oxygen saturation, and body movement. Together, these signals provide a comprehensive picture of the body's physiological state throughout sleep.

---

## Scientific Conclusion

Brain oscillations are measured primarily through EEG, which records synchronized electrical activity from large populations of cortical neurons. The frequency and amplitude of these oscillations provide reliable physiological indicators of the brain's functional state. When combined with additional physiological signals in polysomnography, brain oscillations allow accurate identification of sleep stages and many other neurological conditions.

## Why this matters for our product

This question establishes one of the most important engineering foundations of the project.

Our future TMR system will never observe memory consolidation directly. Instead, it must infer the brain's internal state from measurable physiological signals.

EEG represents the most direct measurement of brain activity and is considered the gold standard for sleep-stage classification. However, many consumer wearable devices do not measure EEG continuously. Instead, they estimate sleep stages using indirect physiological signals such as heart rate, heart-rate variability (HRV), respiration, movement, skin temperature, and blood oxygen levels.

One of the central research goals of this project is therefore to determine whether these consumer-accessible physiological signals can approximate laboratory-grade sleep-stage detection with sufficient accuracy to support reliable Targeted Memory Reactivation.
