# RQ-018

## Question

**How should memories be encoded during wakefulness to maximize successful Targeted Memory Reactivation?**

---

## Status

**Answered**

---

## Why this question matters

Targeted Memory Reactivation begins long before the user falls asleep. During sleep, the brain can only reactivate memories that were successfully encoded during wakefulness. Therefore, understanding how information should be learned and associated with sensory cues is one of the most important requirements for building an effective TMR system.

Without a properly established memory trace and a reliable cue-memory association, sleep cueing cannot selectively reactivate the intended memory.

---

## Write the answer in your own words with Scientific evidence

Successful Targeted Memory Reactivation depends on the quality of the **encoding phase**, during which new information is learned while being consistently paired with a specific sensory cue such as a sound or an odor. This pairing creates an associative link between the external cue and the neural representation of the learned information.

Scientific evidence suggests that memory encoding should satisfy several fundamental conditions.

First, the information must be **actively learned during wakefulness**. TMR cannot strengthen memories that were never successfully encoded because sleep primarily consolidates existing memory traces rather than creating new ones.

Second, the learner should maintain **focused attention and active engagement** during learning. Memories receiving greater attention during encoding are more likely to become prioritized by the brain's natural consolidation mechanisms during subsequent sleep.

Third, the sensory cue should be **consistently and uniquely associated** with the target memory throughout the learning process. The cue functions as a retrieval signal that can later activate the corresponding neural representation during sleep. Neutral and easily distinguishable auditory or olfactory cues are commonly used because they can be presented during sleep without requiring conscious processing.

Finally, the association between the cue and the learned information should remain stable across repeated learning trials. Stronger cue-memory associations increase the probability that presentation of the cue during sleep will selectively bias the replay of the intended memory trace.

Current evidence indicates that the effectiveness of TMR depends far more on the quality of the initial encoding than on the number of cue presentations during sleep. Sleep cueing strengthens memories that already exist; it cannot compensate for weak or unsuccessful learning during wakefulness.

---

## Scientific Conclusion

The encoding phase establishes the biological foundation upon which Targeted Memory Reactivation operates. Effective TMR requires that memories are intentionally learned during wakefulness while being reliably paired with distinctive sensory cues under conditions of focused attention. These cue-memory associations provide the biological link that later enables selective memory reactivation during sleep.

---

## Why this matters for our product

This research question defines the first stage of our future TMR pipeline.

Before any sleep monitoring begins, the application must guide users through a structured learning session in which each learning item is paired with a carefully selected sensory cue. The software must ensure that users actively attend to the material and that every cue is consistently associated with its corresponding memory.

This means that our product consists of two interconnected systems rather than a sleep-only application:

1. **Wakefulness Module** — presents learning material, monitors user engagement, and creates reliable cue-memory associations.
2. **Sleep Module** — detects the appropriate sleep stage and re-presents previously associated sensory cues to bias the brain's natural memory consolidation processes.

The scientific evidence therefore demonstrates that successful Targeted Memory Reactivation begins with high-quality learning during wakefulness. The effectiveness of the sleep phase depends directly on the strength of the associations established during encoding.
